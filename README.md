# Cavity Phase Estimation — Directional Observability & Quantum Coherent Observer

**Quantum control · Linear observer · Homodyne detection · Kalman filtering**

## Overview

We study the problem of estimating a **hidden stochastic optical phase** $\varphi(t)$ from the noisy output of a balanced homodyne detector coupled to an open optical cavity. The phase is never measured directly — it must be reconstructed from its dynamical imprint on the intracavity quadratures.

Three increasingly realistic settings are addressed:

1. **Scalar benchmark** — direct Ornstein–Uhlenbeck phase observation via a linearised homodyne record; Kalman–Bucy vs. Luenberger.
2. **Single cavity** — the phase drives the $p$-quadrature indirectly; finite-horizon directional observability metric $J_\varphi(T)$.
3. **Augmented two-cavity (QCO)** — an auxiliary cavity is coupled before detection; regime-dependent visibility/noise trade-off; adaptive EKF.

### Hidden phase model

The hidden optical phase follows an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process:

$$d\varphi(t) = -\lambda\,\varphi(t)\,dt + \sqrt{q_\varphi}\,dW_\varphi(t), \qquad \lambda,\,q_\varphi > 0 $$

Its stationary variance is ${Var}[\varphi] = q_\varphi / (2\lambda)$.

### Linearised cavity quadrature model

After moving to the rotating frame and linearising around the coherent operating point $\alpha = \varepsilon/(\gamma/2 + i\omega)$, the intracavity quadratures $x = [q,\,p]^\top$ satisfy:

$$dx(t) = A\,x(t)\,dt + B_\varphi\,\varphi(t)\,dt + G\,dW_x(t)$$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} -\gamma/2 & \omega \\ -\omega & -\gamma/2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B_\varphi = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ g_\varphi \end{pmatrix}.$$

The phase only drives the $p$-quadrature this is the structural origin of the indirect estimation problem.

### Augmented model

Stacking $X_a = [q,\, p,\, \varphi]^\top$ yields:

$$dX_a = A_a X_a\,dt + G_a\,dW_a, \qquad A_a = \begin{pmatrix} -\gamma/2 & \omega & 0 \\ -\omega & -\gamma/2 & g_\varphi \\ 0 & 0 & -\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

### Homodyne measurement

The balanced homodyne detector at local-oscillator phase $\theta$ produces:

$$dY(t) = C_a X_a(t)\,dt + \sigma_v\,dV(t), \qquad C_a = \sqrt{\kappa_m}\,[\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta,\;0].$$

The structural zero $C_a e_\varphi = 0$ confirms: the phase is never directly visible.

## Key Contribution — Directional Observability Metric

We define the **finite-horizon directional phase-observability metric**:

$$J_\varphi(T) \;=\; e_\varphi^\top W_o(T)\,e_\varphi \;=\; \int_0^T \bigl\|C_a\,e^{A_a t}\,e_\varphi\bigr\|^2\,dt$$

where $W_o(T) = \int_0^T e^{A_a^\top t} C_a^\top C_a e^{A_a t}\,dt$ is the observability Gramian and $e_\varphi = [0,0,1]^\top$.

This quantity is exactly the output energy generated by a unit perturbation in the phase direction. It yields a directional Cramér–Rao bound:

$${Var}\!\left[\hat\alpha_0 - \alpha_0\right] \;\geq\; \frac{\sigma_v^2}{J_\varphi(T)}$$

In closed form (resonant case $\omega = 0$, $\mu = \gamma/2$):

$$J_\varphi(\infty) = \frac{g_\varphi^2\,\kappa_m\,\sin^2\!\theta}{2\lambda\cdot(\gamma/2)\cdot(\lambda + \gamma/2)}$$

### Resonant visibility condition

The phase can influence the output at first order if and only if:

$$g_\varphi \sin\theta \neq 0$$

i.e., the homodyne angle must project onto the phase-sensitive $p$-quadrature ($\theta \approx \pi/2$ is optimal; $\theta = 0$ is structurally blind).

### Steady-state variance decomposition

At steady state, the Kalman phase-error variance satisfies:

$$[P_\infty]_{\varphi\varphi} = \underbrace{\frac{q_\varphi}{2\lambda}}_{\text{open-loop OU variance}} - \underbrace{\frac{\kappa_m\,p_{\theta\varphi}^2}{2\lambda\,\sigma_v^2}}_{\text{measurement correction}}$$

where $p_{\theta\varphi} = \cos\theta\,p_{13} + \sin\theta\,p_{23}$ is determined implicitly by the full Riccati equation. This shows why $J_\varphi(T)$ is an **intra-family predictor** but does not produce a universal RMSE collapse across mixed stochastic regimes.

## Two-Cavity Architecture (QCO)

An auxiliary cavity (index $o$) is coupled to the sensing cavity (index $s$) before detection:

$$dX_Q = A_Q X_Q\,dt + G_Q\,dW_Q, \qquad A_Q = \begin{pmatrix} A_s & K_{s\leftarrow o} & B_s \\ K_{o\leftarrow s} & A_o & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

The information pathway becomes $\varphi \to p_s \to x_s \leftrightarrow x_o \to Y$.

We characterise the trade-off via:

$$\Delta J_\varphi = \frac{J_\varphi^{\text{aug}}(T) - J_\varphi^{\text{plant}}(T)}{\max\!\left(J_\varphi^{\text{plant}}(T),\,\varepsilon\right)}, \qquad \Delta\text{RMSE} = \frac{\text{RMSE}_{\text{plant}} - \text{RMSE}_{\text{aug}}}{\text{RMSE}_{\text{plant}}}$$

The auxiliary cavity is beneficial in **geometry-dominated regimes** (large $g_\varphi$, moderate $\gamma$, $\theta \approx \pi/2$) and detrimental when it mainly injects noise or creates weakly observable modes.
